**The quantum computing job market is small but rapidly growing, with a significant talent shortage driving high demand and competitive compensation.** It's still an emerging field (NISQ-era hardware and early applications), so absolute job numbers are modest compared to mainstream tech, but growth projections are strong and the skills gap creates opportunities for qualified candidates.

### Current State (as of mid-2026)
- **Global workforce**: Roughly 14,500 professionals in pure-play quantum companies as of 2024, with thousands more in supporting roles at big tech, consulting, finance, and government.
- **Job postings**: Quantum-related roles have seen explosive percentage growth (e.g., 450% increase in postings 2018–2023; tripled in the US from 2011–2024; 180–270% surges in recent years). Absolute numbers remain in the low thousands globally at any time, but listings are active.
- **Open roles**: Estimates suggest several thousand to ~7,400+ global openings (including internships) in recent data, with major activity in the US, UK, and Europe.
- **Talent shortage**: Demand significantly outstrips supply. Projections indicate only one qualified candidate per 3 positions in some analyses. McKinsey and others highlight a growing gap, with calls for 10,000+ new skilled workers in the near term.

### Growth Projections
- **By 2030**: ~250,000 quantum-related jobs globally (broader ecosystem including adjacent roles).
- **By 2035**: Up to 840,000 jobs in some forecasts. Regional examples include ~191,000 in the US Midwest (Illinois/Wisconsin/Indiana) alone.
- Market growth (driving hiring): Quantum computing market projected at strong CAGRs (30–40%+ in various reports), reaching billions by the late 2020s and supporting broader economic impact.

Hiring is shifting from pure research toward **applied quantum engineering**, hybrid classical-quantum roles, software/integration, error correction, and domain-specific applications (e.g., chemistry, finance, cryptography).

### Salaries
Salaries are high due to scarcity:
- **US averages**: Quantum engineers ~$125k–$180k base. Senior/specialized roles $160k–$200k+, with top total compensation (especially at FAANG/big tech) reaching $300k–$400k including equity.
- **Entry-level**: Often $100k–$140k.
- **Median/overall**: Around $166k in some data; strong annual growth (e.g., 10–12% in recent years vs. 3–5% in broader tech).
- Non-PhD engineering/software roles in the ecosystem also pay well (often $110k–$150k+).

### Key Companies and Locations
**Major hirers**: IBM (largest in US), Google, Microsoft, Amazon/AWS, Quantinuum, IonQ, Rigetti, PsiQuantum, plus defense, consulting, and startups.

**Hot locations**: US (Bay Area, Boston, Chicago, New York), UK (strong European hub), Canada, Europe, and growing government initiatives.

### Required Skills and Education
- **Core**: Strong background in physics, math (linear algebra, probability), computer science. Python + quantum SDKs (Qiskit, Cirq, etc.).
- **Roles vary**:
  - Hardware: PhD-level physicists/engineers (cryogenics, optics, fabrication).
  - Software/algorithms: Strong coding + quantum info science.
  - Applied/hybrid: Transferable skills from classical computing, engineering, or domain expertise (chemistry, finance) — many roles don't require a quantum PhD.
- Over half of jobs may require only a bachelor's with relevant skills; PhDs dominate research/hardware leadership.
- Entry is competitive; hands-on experience (internships, open-source quantum projects, university programs) helps.

### Challenges and Outlook
- **High barrier for pure roles**, especially without advanced degrees or experience. The market favors those who can deliver on real programs rather than hype.
- **Positive drivers**: Government investment (e.g., US National Quantum Initiative), big tech cloud access, and early commercial use cases in optimization, simulation, and post-quantum crypto.
- Not everyone needs to be a "quantum physicist"—adjacent skills in software, engineering, cryogenics, and classical systems are increasingly valuable.

**Bottom line**: It's a high-reward, competitive niche with excellent pay and security for those who break in. The field is transitioning from academia-heavy to industry-applied, creating broader opportunities through 2030+. If you're entering, focus on strong fundamentals, practical coding/hardware skills, and networking via quantum hubs or programs. The talent shortage means motivated candidates with the right preparation have a strong edge.
